The hydrosphere is made up


The hydrosphere is made up of 370 quintillion gallons of water, which is 97% of the Earth, however, only 0.01% of that water is fresh water. In addition, it is important to keep that water fresh considering its limitation. This website discusses some of the factors that may affect the hydrosphere in reference to NASA and the NRF SAEON.

Climate change is the most influencing factor that affect the hydrosphere is the significant variation of average weather becoming for example, warmer, wetter or drier over a long period of time.  It plays a big role in water contamination and water stress.
Warmer temperatures make life difficult for sea creatures, many fish move poleward in search of cooler waters implicating the fishing industry. They also alter major ocean current because temperatures drive atmospheric circulation patterns, this could change weather patterns all over the world and make rainfall more variable and less predictable from year to year

What's the story behind our product?

The Partnership for Observation of the Global Ocean (POGO) and the Alfred-Wegener Institut Helmholtz-Zentrum fur Polar- Meeresforschung (AWI) partnered with Educational Passages to bring a Mini-boat Programme to schools and communities around the world. A mini-boat that will be deployed in South Atlantic Ocean.
From Luhlaza High School we named our group as Ocean Angels and we were innovative enough by installing sensor packages on our boat which can transfer collected data from from the path taken by the boat on the ocean and transfer it to the NASA Satellites, allowing the school learners to track of their own vessel, and that of the others interests drawn in the project and learn about ocean currents, weather, and how the climate change affect the water-cycle and on the availability of fresh water.
 The collected data is available on our website where one can make themselves comfortable to dig in on some information about waves, currents of the region of their choice.

Know more about Marine Science career paths

MARINE BIOLOGIST Position
An example of a Marine Biologist is Thomas Mtontsi who is a scientist who explore the mysteries of life in the oceans and their interactions with humans and the environment.
Physical Oceanography Position
It is a study of physical conditions and processes within the ocean such waves, currents, eddies, and the interactions of the atmosphere and the ocean. 
Marine and Environmental Geochemist Position
Marine Geochemistry studies all processes that influence the chemistry in the water. The chemical cycles in the oceans are closely related to processes in the atmosphere and terrestrial systems as well as to biological, and recently anthropogenic, activity.
